Psychoanalysis Research

What Was the Major Impact of Psychoanalysis?

In the early 1880’s, Dr. Josepsh Breur of Vienna, Austria was treating a woman with severe hysteria. He found that her symptoms were connected with disturbing events that she experienced in the past while nursing her dying father. He used hypnosis to induce her to recall and act out her forgotten experiences, so that her symptoms dissapeared- a process known as the “Cathatic Method.” Although this was the catalyst to the study of psychoanalysis, a method of treating mental disorders, Dr. Sigmaund Freud found that the cures from the Cathartic Method weren’t permanant. So, he replaced this method with “Free Association.” As Freud and Breur published many cases, they developed a plethora of methods under the catagory of psychoanalysis. Most of these methods would take many sessions throughout many years to work effectively. There are numerous methods that were, and still are used. But, they all had the same goal. This was to bring unconscious material into consciousness and enhance the functioning of the ego, helping each individual become less controlled by biological drives or demands of the superego. In other words, patients became clensed of their unconscious tramas.

How Will Psychoanlysis be Used in the Future?

Psychoanalysis is on a major decline. Though many new versions of Freud's are being modernized and used, the field is still dying. The true psychoanalysis developed during the Industrial Revolution is now irrelevant, and the future will most likely have even less psychoanalysis usage. replaced with many new message in theories. A highly popular form of therapy is Cognitive-Based therapy. This is relating more to solving the current issues with the patient to improve cognitive abilities and functioning. This type of therapy is preferred by many, mainly because it doesn't take as many years as psychoanalysis to be put into effect. It also doesn't involve bringing back painful experiences of the past. Thus, although psychoanalysis is good in theory, and sometimes in result, most patients do not want to go through the long and painful process. Furthermore, if psychoanalysis keeps its same pattern and is not saved by psychologiststs, it will continue on its decline.

What is the Modern Day Impact of Psychoanalysis?

Today, psychoanalysis is one of the most controversial approaches to psychotherapy. the movement has slowed drastically, but many new and modified methods of Freud's are still considered valid. Albiet it isn’t a highly popular concept, it is still being studied and debated over. Ink Blot, Free Association, Dream Analysis, Resistance Analysis, Transference Analysis, and the Freudian Slip are the accepted approaches that have been developed up until this point in time. While these approaches are not very common and have been mostly replaced with methods like Cognitive-Based Therapy (CBT), there are still many researches that consider them to be highly effective. One paper found that long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (LTPP) is more effective than less intensive forms of psychotherapy in treating complex mental disorders. Another found that, on a larger scale, LTPP had a much higher rate of effectiveness targeting problems and general personality functioning than shorter forms of psychotherapy. Furthermore, it was said to have a more positive impact than other modern healthcare therapies, with improvement continuing after treatment. Another study concluded that the benefits of short-term psychodynamic therapies are equivalent to those acquired from antidepressants and CBT. Lastly, and a study on panic disorder, 49 adults with panic disorder were given either psychodynamic treatment or relaxation training. Those given the treatment showed a significantly greater reduction of panic symptoms than those who received relaxation training, and even great improvement in psychosocial functioning. This shows the large impact psychoanalysis has had on the modern world, though it may seem to be an outdated concept.
